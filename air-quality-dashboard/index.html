01 — Problem

Beijing has a serious air quality problem. The data exists. The story doesn't.

Raw sensor CSV files across 12 stations and 5 years tell you nothing useful on their own. The goal was to build a dashboard that translates that raw data into patterns a policy analyst or researcher could actually act on.

The dataset contains hourly measurements of PM2.5, PM10, SO₂, NO₂, CO, and O₃ alongside meteorological readings (temperature, pressure, dew point, wind speed, rainfall) from 12 stations spread across urban, suburban, and rural zones.

Three core questions shaped the analysis:

  • Temporal patterns — When is air quality worst? Is there a seasonal cycle? A weekly one? An hour-of-day cycle?
  • Meteorological influence — Does wind speed actually disperse pollutants? How does rainfall interact with PM2.5 readings?
  • Spatial inequality — Which stations consistently record the highest pollutant levels, and why?

02 — Data & Approach

From raw CSV to EPA-grade AQI — the pipeline

Before any visualization could happen, the raw measurements needed to be converted into a standardized AQI scale using the EPA breakpoint method.

AQI Calculation

The EPA uses a piecewise linear interpolation formula to convert raw pollutant concentrations into sub-index values. The final AQI is the maximum of all sub-indices:

AQI = max(AQI_PM2.5, AQI_PM10, AQI_O3, AQI_CO, AQI_NO2, AQI_SO2)

Each pollutant maps to one of six categories: Good (0–50), Moderate (51–100), Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups (101–150), Unhealthy (151–200), Very Unhealthy (201–300), Hazardous (301+).

Statistical Testing

Spearman rank correlation was used for meteorological relationships (non-parametric, appropriate for non-normal distributions). Wind speed and rainfall showed negative correlation with pollutant levels — dispersal and washout effects respectively.

Chi-square tests for categorical associations: zone type vs AQI category (χ² = 4,600.68, p < 0.001) and season vs PM2.5 level (χ² = 11,819.36, p < 0.001). Both statistically significant — zone and season genuinely predict air quality.

Spatial Priority Scoring

Each station received a composite priority score using Z-score normalized values:

priority = 0.5 × PM2.5_z + 0.35 × AQI_z + 0.15 × gas_index_z

This weighted score drives the geospatial map — circle markers are sized by PM2.5 level, colored by priority tier, so the worst-polluted stations are immediately visible.

03 — Dashboard Features

Five views, one coherent picture of Beijing air quality

  • Header KPIs — Real-time current AQI, dominant pollutant, and weather profile at a glance. The entry point that tells the user whether today is "Good" or "Hazardous" before they explore anything.
  • History Trends — Daily, monthly, and yearly AQI views switchable via toggle. Heatmap shows hour-of-day × month patterns — winter mornings are consistently the worst.
  • Dynamic Insights — Trend analysis surface, critical period detection, seasonal PM2.5 patterns, and extreme event flagging. Automatically highlights the worst 10% of readings as "critical periods."
  • Meteorology Analysis — Seasonal distribution box plots, Spearman correlation heatmap, and wind rose diagram. Answers: does rain help? (yes) does high pressure trap pollution? (yes).
  • Geospatial Priority Map — Interactive Folium map with circle markers. Station size = PM2.5 level; color = priority tier. Urban cluster stations clearly show higher pollution than rural zones.
  • Categorical Analysis — Zone type vs AQI category contingency table with chi-square output. Confirms statistically that where a station is located (urban/suburban/rural) predicts its air quality category.

05 — Technical Decisions

Three choices that shaped the analysis

Spearman over Pearson for meteorological correlations

Meteorological data (wind speed, rainfall) doesn't follow a normal distribution. Pearson correlation assumes linearity and normality — both violated here. Spearman rank correlation is more robust for this kind of skewed, sensor-based data. The resulting correlation heatmap is more honest.

Composite priority score instead of raw PM2.5 ranking

Ranking stations purely by PM2.5 misses the full picture. A station might have high PM2.5 but low AQI (if other pollutants are clean) or vice versa. The weighted composite (50% PM2.5, 35% AQI, 15% gas index) gives a more complete picture of which stations need policy attention most.

IQR-based outlier detection instead of z-score cutoffs

Sensor data includes genuine extreme events (dust storms, industrial incidents) that look like outliers statistically but are real. IQR method was chosen for outlier flagging to be more conservative — preserving real extreme readings while still identifying sensor malfunctions.

Tiga area spesifik yang perlu diperbaiki:

Feedback #1 — Pertanyaan Bisnis

Pertanyaan 2, 3, dan 4 belum memenuhi aspek Time-bound dari prinsip SMART. Pertanyaan seperti "kapan waktu-waktu kritis?" valid secara konsep, tapi tidak terukur secara analitis. Menambahkan "selama periode 2013–2017" mengubahnya dari samar menjadi testable.

Feedback #2 — Kedalaman EDA

Analisis univariat kurang skewness, kurtosis, Q-Q plot, dan uji normalitas Shapiro-Wilk. Bagian multivariat perlu scatter plot dengan regression line, pairplot, dan violin plot. Bagian kategorikal belum ada uji chi-square untuk memvalidasi asosiasi yang terlihat secara visual. Deteksi outlier berbasis IQR per polutan juga perlu didokumentasikan.

Feedback #3 — Interaktivitas Dashboard

Dashboard Streamlit perlu filter waktu dinamis dan kontrol interaktif agar pengguna bisa mengeksplorasi data sendiri, bukan hanya melihat output statis.

Pengembangan ke Depan

Berdasarkan feedback reviewer dan arah alami analisis ini, berikut yang akan dikerjakan pada iterasi berikutnya:

Komponen Prediktif

Tambahkan model forecasting AQI berbasis kondisi cuaca (angin, tekanan, suhu). Dashboard saat ini menjelaskan pola historis — langkah berikutnya adalah memprediksi hari-hari berisiko sebelum terjadi.

Mode Perbandingan Stasiun

Filter interaktif untuk membandingkan dua stasiun secara berdampingan — berguna untuk analisis kebijakan zonasi antara area urban dan rural.

Alert Otomatis Periode Kritis

Logika threshold PM2.5 yang secara otomatis menandai dan merangkum "critical periods" — sehingga pengguna tidak perlu mencarinya manual di heatmap.

SMART sebagai Standar

Setiap pertanyaan bisnis di proyek mendatang akan diformulasikan dengan Time-bound eksplisit sejak awal — bukan sebagai revisi, tapi sebagai cara berpikir default.
